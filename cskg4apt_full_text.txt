CSKG4APT: A Cybersecurity Knowledge
Graph for Advanced Persistent Threat
Organization Attribution
Yitong Ren, Yanjun Xiao, Yinghai Zhou
, Zhiyong Zhang, and Zhihong Tian
, Senior Member, IEEE
Abstract—Open-source cyber threat intelligence (OSCTI) is becoming more inﬂuential in obtaining current network security
information. Most studies on cyber threat intelligence (CTI) focus on automating the extraction of threat entities from public sources
that describe attack events. The cybersecurity knowledge graph aims to change the expression of threat knowledge so that security
researchers can accurately and efﬁciently obtain various types of threat information for preliminary intelligent decisions. The attribution
technology can not only assist security analysts in detecting advanced persistent threats, but can also identify the same threat from
different attack events. Therefore, it is important to trace the attack threat actor. In this study, we used the knowledge graph technology,
considered the latest research on cyber threat attack attribution, and thoroughly examined key related technologies and theories in the
process of constructing and applying the advanced persistent threat (APT) knowledge graph from OSCTI. We designed a cybersecurity
platform named CSKG4APT based on a knowledge graph. Inspired by the theory of ontology, we constructed CSKG4APTas an APT
knowledge graph model based on real APTattack scenarios. We then designed an APT threat knowledge extraction algorithm for
completing and updating the knowledge graph using deep learning and expert knowledge. Finally, we proposed a practical APTattack
attribution method with attribution and countermeasures. CSKG4APT is not a passive defense method in traditional network
confrontation but one that integrates a large amount of fragmented intelligence and can actively adjust its defense strategy. It lays the
foundation for further dominance in network attack and defense.
Index Terms—Cyber threat intelligence, cybersecurity knowledge graph, APTorganization attribution, diamond model

INTRODUCTION

WING to the rapid growth of computer data and the
development
of
artiﬁcial
intelligence
technologies,
cyber attacks have become increasingly sophisticated and
diversiﬁed while cybersecurity has become more challeng-
ing. A general term in the industry is advanced persistent
threat (APT) attacks, which was ﬁrst proposed by the U.S.
Department of Defense and the U.S. Air Force. APT attacks
refer to the persistent attacks against important assets of
enterprises or key national infrastructure as well as impor-
tant government departments by advanced technical means,
hacker organizations, and national backgrounds. An APT
attack is a long-term, persistent network attack on a speciﬁc
target using advanced and hidden attack methods [1].
According to a Kaspersky APT report [2], the style and threat
level of APT attacks have evolved in the past year. The
attacks vary (e.g., supply-chain attacks and ﬁrmware vulner-
abilities), and no single cybersecurity vendor completely
identiﬁes the activities of all threat actors. Fig. 1 presents the
major APT attacks recorded in the past ten years.
Recent APT attacks, as illustrated in Fig. 1, have proven
that traditional network security defenses have become inef-
fective against APT attacks. The diminished ineffectiveness
lies in the information asymmetry between the attacker and
defender. The attacker can easily obtain the identity informa-
tion of the defender, who would ﬁnd it difﬁcult to obtain any
information about the attacker before the attack occurs. An
active collection of information about network threats is a
major way of preventing network attacks and the rapidly
evolving threats of information imbalance between attackers
and defenders. Cyber threat intelligence (CTI) has also
become prominent in enhancing the ability to resist network
threats [3]. More cybersecurity personnel and organizations
are now willing to exchange and share CTI to improve their
cybersecurity defense capabilities as threat intelligence
drives network security defense in the ﬁeld of cybersecurity.
Timely intelligence sharing and accurate intelligence analy-
sis are the keys to effectively responding to network threats.
More intuitively, the sharing and utilization of CTI is a kind
of advanced security protection strategy of ”space for time.”
By proactively acquiring existing or potential network
threats and limiting the attack time and scope, the threat
response time is considerably shortened, thus changing the
asymmetry in the process of offensive and defensive

the structured indicators of compromise (IoC), which identi-

Examples of IoC are hashes in malware samples or malicious

and highly unstructured natural language.
Many security researchers and groups have explored
OSCTI reports that address unstructured text. Here, extract-
ing entities [5], [6] and relationships [7], [8] from OSCTI
reports is an important step. Thus, automated tools have
been designed to extract attack information from OSCTI
reports; the tools can automatically extract threat indicators
or IoC from public sources that describe attack events to ana-
lyze CTI. Google launched a search engine service based on
knowledge graphs on May 17, 2012[11]. After recent years of

sharing of CTI by security researchers while addressing the
tedious and messy process of handling CTI data. However,
most studies on entity extraction in knowledge graphs are
based on English, whereas obvious differences exist between
Chinese and English [15]. Meanwhile, it is difﬁcult to update
the labeling information of the IoC because of the growing
threat information. Therefore, an important research direc-
tion is the repair of the IoC information labels and the inter-
dependent relationships in OSCTI reports. However, only a
few studies have investigated the application of attack infor-
mation after its extraction and construction. In the research
of APT attack scenarios, traceable graph attack detection (by
learning the attack behavior) is gradually replacing anomaly
detection (by learning the user’s normal behavior through
audit logs) as a more effective method. ATLAS[16] learned
the sequence of attacking and non-attacking behaviors
simultaneously and constructed attack events by associating
entities with events. However, it is equally important to
study the attribution of attack events compared with attack
detection and inference using logs or provenance graphs in
the ﬁeld of APT attack detection. Without conﬁrmation of
the identity and behavioral biases of the threat actor, it is
impossible to predict a similar occurrence.
To address the above problems, the Cyberspace Insti-
tute of Advanced Technology of Guangzhou University,
in conjunction with Nsfocus PINGXING Laboratory and
FUYING Laboratory, established CSKG4APT, a cyber-
space threat modeling platform based on a knowledge
graph. The model extracts relevant threat information
entities by collecting and analyzing the description infor-
mation and logical relationship of the APT attack organi-
zation in OSCTI. By combining STIX[13], CYBOX[17], and
other threat intelligence standards, the group constructed
an APT cybersecurity attribution knowledge graph. The
entity extraction algorithm for bilingual CTI reports was
designed to update knowledge in CSKG4APT. Finally,
the diamond model and threat hunting were applied in
CSKG4APT to propose a scheme of APT attack organiza-
tion portrait tracking.
The main contributions of this study are as follows:
1. Strong knowledge representation ability. Cyber threat
knowledge reﬂects cyber threat information in different
languages and from multiple dimensions. We designed
CSKG4APT, a cybersecurity threat modeling platform based
on a knowledge graph, which can effectively fuse multi-
dimensional knowledge elements and ensure a comprehen-
sive analysis of various associations in APT threat knowl-
edge. CSKG4APT is a cybersecurity knowledge graph model
for APT organization attribution based on the theory of ontol-
ogy and built through the collection and analysis of APT
organization information in OSCTI; STIX[13], CYBOX[17],
and other threat intelligence data standards are also incorpo-
rated. CSKG4APT shows a complete APT knowledge graph,
which combines tactical sets (attack characteristics and mod-
els), weapon sets (threats and vulnerabilities of attack tools),
and asset sets (high-risk and key assets) for an effective map-
ping of cybersecurity elements in CTI.
2. Automatic knowledge extraction. To update APT threat
knowledge graphs, we proposed an algorithm to recognize
threat knowledge using bidirectional encoder representations
from transformers (BERT) for entity extraction from bilingual

RELATED WORKS
Recently, researchers have conducted lots of studies on
APT, CTI, and threat knowledge graph, which are summa-
rized in Table 1 according to the ﬁelds and methods.
2.1
Cyber Threat Intelligence
CTI Standards. Many CTI standards and frameworks based
on different demands have been established to facilitate the
automatic generation and sharing of CTI. Examples of rep-
resentative CTI standards are STIX[13], TAXII [19], CyBox
[17], MISP[21], and OpenIOC [20].
CTI Analysis. Recently, security researchers and groups
have attempted to automate the extraction of threat entities
from open sources describing attack events. Field of statistical
machine learning: Mulwad[22] et al. used support vector
machine classiﬁers to extract concepts about vulnerabilities,
attacks, and threats related to attacks. Joshi[23] et al. used a
conditional random ﬁeld (CRF) approach to identify and clas-
sify references to entities and concepts. Field of deep learning:
In the published SemEval-2018, Manikandan[24] et al. used a
CNN–CRF model to identify entities related to malicious soft-
ware. Dionsio[25] et al. constructed a BiLSTM–CRF named
entity recognition model to identify named entities from net-
work security tweets on Twitter. Gasmi[26] et al. exploited an
LSTM–CRF model to extract network security concepts and
entities. Satyapanich[27] et al. designed CASIE, a system that
uses the BiLSTM model and an attention mechanism for clas-
sifying cybersecurity entities. Li[28] et al. proposed an atten-
tion-mechanism-based BiLSTM–CRF model for the entity
recognition of network security texts. HINCTI[29] proposed
a heterogeneous graph convolutional network method based
on MIIS measurement to identify the threat types of infra-
structure nodes in CTI and to complete relevant IoC informa-
tion. Field of deep natural language processing (NLP): Liao[30]
et al. developed iACE, a system that transforms the task of
extracting IoC into a graph similarity problem. If an IOC has
a similar graph structure to that of the training set, it is identi-
ﬁed as a deﬁnite IoC, meaning iACE can automatically
extract IoC from CTI and capture their context. Husari[31]
et al. proposed TTPDrill, which uses NLP and information
retrieval to extract threat actions from unstructured CTI text.
Zhao[32] et al. designed TIMiner, an IOC extractor with
domain labels, which can automatically extract IoC and cor-
responding domain (region information) labels in network
security texts. Satvat[33] et al. proposed Extractor to automat-
ically extract attack behaviors from CTI reports using NLP
technology; the authors also exploited semantic role labeling
for semantic analysis to understand the relationships among
attack behaviors. Finally, the unstructured text is output in
the form of a provenance graph.
2.2
Cybersecurity Knowledge Graph
The cybersecurity knowledge graph enhances the presenta-
tion of threat knowledge to enable security researchers
accurately and efﬁciently obtain various threat information
for preliminary intelligent decision-making.
In terms of academic research, Zhao[34] et al. deﬁned dif-
ferent ontologies based on expert knowledge to describe the
relationships between entities. Kiesling[35] built SEPSES
knowledge graph (SKG), a knowledge graph for cybersecur-
ity, and demonstrated how to generate security alerts from
Snort and connect them to SKG for a deeper understanding
of ongoing cyber threats. Deng[36] et al. constructed a knowl-
edge graph of cybersecurity practices for students by leverag-
ing big data and NLP techniques. Using the constructed
knowledge graph, students can browse and search for

2.3
Cyber Threat Attribution
The technology of attribution can not only assist security ana-
lysts in detecting advanced persistent threats, but can also
establish a connection between threats from different attack
events. Noor [43]et al. extracted high-level IoC from CTI docu-
ments; converted the IoC into a CTA–TTP correlation matrix,
which is used as the input of the classiﬁer to train the model;
and made predictions and attributions. Qiang[44] et al. pro-
posed a network attack attribution framework based on threat
intelligence, which achieves a certain accuracy in the attribu-
tion results. The triangle model proposed by Warikoocite[45]
et al. performs the attribution of an attack by extracting and
comparing three indicators (sector, tools, TTPs) for credible
results. Hettema[47] et al. analyzed the cognitive state from a
philosophical perspective to reduce the cognitive load during
attribution while improving the reliability of the attribution
results. Sahoo[48] et al. extracted features from the perspec-
tives of malware opcode, bytecode, and header and trained
multiple machine learning algorithms to build a multi-per-
spective heuristic analysis model. Belaoued[49] et al. pro-
posed a machine learning-based heuristic analysis method
that utilizes the application programming interface and tech-
nical PE features invoked by malware programs to classify
malware ﬁles.

SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
The overall architecture of CSKG4APT consists of four
parts: information acquisition, threat intelligence analysis,
construction of threat knowledge graph model, and applica-
tion of CSKG4APT for APT attack organization attribution.
The architecture is illustrated in Fig. 2.
Through CSKG4APT, a uniﬁed language can be estab-
lished to describe the behavior and characteristics of different
APT organizations. The system also contains the construction
of a knowledge base for APT organizations as well as knowl-
edge updates. Moreover, to comprehensively understand the
attackers and their behavioral biases and consequently design
effective defensive measures, CSKG4APT can be used for
attack simulation, tracking of known attacking organizations,
and identiﬁcation of unknown attacking organizations. Each
of these is described in detail below.
3.1
Construction of Cybersecurity
Knowledge Graph
The ﬁeld of cyber threat intelligence and related security
elements have many relatively mature standard systems,
such as the structured threat information expression (STIX)
[13]. For speciﬁc security elements, the corresponding stan-
dard speciﬁcations are: common attack pattern enumeration
and classiﬁcation (CAPEC) [14], malware attribute enumer-
ation and characterization (MAEC) [53], common vulner-
abilities & exposures (CVE)[54], etc.
Since the data source of this study is an unstructured text
report collected from various sources, the knowledge type
is improved. CSKG4APT focuses on attacking organizations
(such as APT organizations, malicious code families) and
implements a set of APT organization portrait tracing
schemes through the knowledge of the organization’s tech-
nical level (attack tools, attack methods, vulnerabilities,
malicious samples), network infrastructure (IP, domain,

3.1.1
Type of Entity
The construction of threat entities comprises two parts:
knowledge type design and dictionary speciﬁcation deﬁni-
tion. Knowledge type design limits the content range of
knowledge graph description, and the knowledge graph
proposed in this study is limited within APT. Dictionary
speciﬁcation restricts the attribute description of knowledge
to unify the expression.
Knowledge Type Design. Fig. 3 illustrates 12 knowledge
types deﬁned in CSKG4APT: Attacker, Attack pattern, Mal-
ware, Vulnerabilities, Target, Infrastructure, Software/tools,
Assets, Campaign, Impact, Defensive strategy, and Reports.
Table 2 details the knowledge types. Each knowledge type
contains several sub-classes. For example, ”Attacker” con-
tains ”threat_actor” and ”organization”; ”Malware” contains
”Backdoor_tools,” ”Dropper_tools,” and ”Exploit_tools.”
Dictionary Speciﬁcation. As a description constraint of dif-
ferent types of knowledge attributes, dictionary speciﬁca-
tion facilitates the uniﬁed expression and understanding of
knowledge and is also the standard of external data fusion
disambiguation. The dictionary speciﬁcation is intended to
describe the properties of the type of knowledge; it also
refers to STIX and various security standards.

Each relationship type contains several sub-classes. For

3.1.3
Case
In the actual scenarios, it is necessary to detect a threat
ontology in the knowledge system, such as SMB remote
code execution vulnerability (CVE-2017-0143). Through the
semantic relationship established by the knowledge graph
and the asset information (server, ﬁrewall, router), the
affected assets (server) are output and relevant disposal
suggestions (close port 445, trafﬁc discard) are recom-
mended. In addition to acquiring situational information,
the threat impact and possible defensive measures are con-
sidered. An example of EternalBlue is illustrated in Fig. 4.
3.2
Collection and Analysis of Threat Information
Since a large amount of advanced threat intelligence currently
exists in unstructured text, CSKG4APT ﬁrst constructs highly
scalable intelligence feeds through intelligence feed conﬁgu-
ration templates. Subsequently, intelligence related to attack-
ers is ﬁltered while irrelevant data are removed. Finally,
multiple threat intelligence types are automatically extracted
by combining knowledge base and NLP technology.
The above method is implemented through three main
functional modules: 1) Collection of original information, 2)
Distinction of intelligence topics, and 3) Automated extrac-
tion of threat intelligence. To achieve high scalability, a gen-
eral collection conﬁguration template is established in the
module of original intelligence collection through the analy-
sis of unstructured intelligence data sources. To ﬁlter irrele-
vant data in intelligence sources, the intelligence topic
distinguishing module takes the attacker as the core and
identiﬁes and distinguishes the original intelligence related

crawling. This module eventually outputs structured data
and unstructured raw report documents as HTML ﬁles.
The crawler framework is optimized and improved using
the Scrapy–Redis framework [50]. The crawler framework
greatly improves the ﬂexibility of the crawler through the
interactive use of the engine, scheduler, downloader,
crawler, pipeline, and middleware. The cumbersome, redun-
dant code writing is also simpliﬁed, and repeated requests
can be reduced by combining Redis for ﬁngerprint dedupli-
cation. This method improves the overall performance and
usability of the crawler through the following middleware.
1) Proxy Middleware. When the client frequently sends
requests to the server, the server collects the IP behavior
and access frequency to identify and prohibit the crawler
from running. Therefore, the CSKG4APT dynamic IP proxy
middleware places the acquired dynamic IP in the Redis
database and randomly obtains the dynamic IP from the
locally built IP pool web application interface to avoid being
banned because of excessively fast access.
2) Headers Middleware. Generally, the server not only identi-
ﬁes the client type from the IP, but also judges it based on the
request header. To better crawl server information, a request
header middleware is written according to the real request
header speciﬁcation, which is used to simulate the process of
various browsers initiating requests. CSKG4APT refers to the
open-source libraries, modiﬁes the request headers for differ-
ent websites and request failures, and disables the request
header settings in the Scrapy download middleware.
3) Retry Middleware. Many errors may occur from the
point of sending a request to the point of receiving the
returned data. According to the application scenario,
CSKG4APT has a retry middleware, which is conﬁgured to
reattempt a request when certain responses are received
and stop attempting when the attempts of a certain resource
reach a certain threshold. The original Intelligence collection
processing is shown in Algorithm 1.

3.2.3
Automated Extraction of Threat Intelligence
The module of automated extraction of threat intelligence
takes the original report as input and uses the normalized
text cleaning method to clean and respond to the hidden
threat intelligence in the input text. The module then com-
bines targeted regularization and named entity recognition
technology to extract threat intelligence. The extracted intel-
ligence types are IP, domain, URL, vulnerabilities, attack
techniques, sample hashes, etc. Subsequently, NLP technol-
ogy is used to identify attackers, software tools, geographic
information, attack targets, and other information. Finally,
formatted threat intelligence data are output.
Analysis and research on original reports from different
sources showed that the extracted original report content
text (HTML/PDF) contains wrong line breaks and advertis-
ing content, which interfere with the extraction of threat
intelligence. Therefore, to improve the accuracy of auto-
mated threat intelligence extraction, the following cleaning
is required:

Remove special symbols on the left or right side of
”.,” ”@,” ”/,” and ”//”;

Transform the keywords ”hxxp” and ”hxxps”;

Record speciﬁc keywords, such as ”contact us,”
related to the recommendation and exclude the
information immediately following these keywords;
and

Interrupt index construction for keywords related to
the end of the article (such as ”about us,” ”team
introduction”) to exclude the fetch errors due to ad
content.
Afterward, the module identiﬁes and extracts common
basic threat intelligence through regular expressions, includ-
ing IP, domain, email, URL, hash, CVE, and ATT&CK. For
cyber threat intelligence texts, to provide strong support for
source tracing and system defense, CSKG4APT has a threat
intelligence named entity recognition system CTI View[51]
based on BERT [52]. Unlike English CTI reports, Chinese CTI
contents are segmented by words and punctuation because
of the particularity of the Chinese language. The CTI View
ﬁrst inputs the labeled data into the BERT layer and then
obtains a pre-trained model and the word vector. Subse-
quently, the word vector is input into the BiLSTM, GRU, and
CRF modules in turn for processing. Finally, ﬁve entities of
attackers, software/tools, industries, regions, and cam-
paigns are extracted, and the proposed method is evaluated
on the dataset.
3.3
APT Organization Attribution
In the past few years, unlike the traditional cyber threat
attribution
with
query
and
association
as
the

CSKG4APT uses the knowledge graph and big data of net-
work space security to design a targeted big data analysis
engine. The engine is based on expert knowledge supple-
mented by artiﬁcial intelligence and is for conducting clue-
based reconnaissance and investigation. It uses the dia-
mond model to attribute APT organizational portraits to
generate more intelligence. The model facilitates the con-
struction of strategic deterrence and defense capabilities in
cybersecurity.
The diamond model was proposed by Caltagirone[57] in
the article, ”The Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis,” in
2013. The model describes the opponent using infrastruc-
ture deployment capabilities for victims, which contain four
basic elements.

Adversary: An adversary is the actor/organization
responsible for utilizing a capability against the vic-
tim to achieve their intent.

Victim: A victim is the target of the adversary and
against whom vulnerabilities and exposures are
exploited and capabilities used.

Capability: The capability feature describes the tools
and/or techniques of the adversary used in the
event.

Infrastructure: The infrastructure feature describes
the physical and/or logical communication struc-
tures the adversary uses to deliver a capability,
maintain control of capabilities (e.g., command-and-
control/C2), and effect results from the victim (e.g.,
exﬁltrate data).
In this study, the diamond model is extended, and the
huge data support of CSKG4APT is used to sketch different
attack events launched by APT attack groups. A portrait of
the prototype diagram is presented in Fig. 6.

This section discusses the limitations of CSKG4APT and the

The ﬁrst limitation is timeliness. Currently, CSKG4APT
cannot realize knowledge completion and automatic con-
struction online for knowledge with heterogeneity and
large-scale unstructured content. Thus, a time window T is
required for collecting APT reports. CSKG4APT is updated
after entity extraction; if the timeliness performance is
enhanced as much as possible, the collected reports might
be too few, resulting in wasted computing resources. If the
time window T is too large, the reports would be sufﬁcient,
but the inﬂuence of CSKG4APT on providing decision assis-
tance for security analysts would be small. Automatic con-
struction and dynamics is also a challenge in the knowledge
graph ﬁeld[59].
The second limitation is relevance. CSKG4APT considers
many standard systems in the CTI, and CAPEC is among
the entity types of Attack_Pattern. However, a semantic gap
exists between the low-semantic IoC and the high-level
view of security operations. We can design a framework for
correlating known attack data with ATT&CK. The security
analyst can have a clearer understanding of the attack event
by using the graph technology to restore the attack scenario
corresponding to the ATT&CK. Moreover, by also consider-
ing the attack gang information accumulated by the knowl-
edge graph, we can investigate methods of using the
existing knowledge in security incidents to complete, infer,
and predict to address the problems of insufﬁciency and
discontinuity in complex attack contexts in CTI reports.
Finally, further analyses are required. As an important
tool used by attackers, malware is also an important
reference for security researchers in threat hunting. We can
learn the behavior preferences of attackers more accurately
by learning the behavior of malicious attacks. Question
answering and recommender systems are also important
research directions.

CONCLUSION
This paper presents CSKG4APT, a cybersecurity threat plat-
form based on a knowledge graph. CSKG4APT constructs
the fragmented information into an APT scene containing
12 node types and seven logical relationships by collecting
and analyzing OSCTI. The platform generates an APT
cybersecurity knowledge graph that complies with STIX,
CYBOX, and other threat intelligence standards. In the
experimental evaluation, our algorithm accurately extracted
entities in English and Chinese CTI reports; we analyzed
the attack organization information in CSKG4APT and dis-
played its portrait. The effect of CSKG4APT on attribution
in a real campaign example was demonstrated to assist
security analysts in making decisions. In the future, we plan
to expand the application module to obtain more accurate
campaign and attribution information.
